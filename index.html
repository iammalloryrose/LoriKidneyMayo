I now spend three days each week connected to a machine that keeps me alive, but robs me of time, energy, and freedom. I've had to cancel trips, miss out on experiences, and put life on hold — all while waiting on a transplant list that could take 5 to 8 more years for someone with my blood type.

I want to live — not just survive. If you are someone with a generous heart who is even slightly open to the idea of being a living kidney donor, please take a moment to explore the 2 links below. A living donation could restore my health and give me the chance to be here, fully, for the people who need me most.

Why I am Seeking a Donor Outside the Family

Polycystic Kidney Disease is a hereditary condition that has deeply affected my family. Out of six siblings in my generation, four have kidney disease, and the other two have already donated their kidneys to my other siblings in need. Many of my nieces were tested but were either not a blood match or were disqualified due to medical reasons.

Both of my children are showing strong signs of carrying the PKD gene and are unable to donate. My husband, too, has been disqualified due to his own health concerns. Because PKD is so widespread in my family, I am now seeking a living donor from outside the family. A selfless gift from a stranger could offer me a second chance at life.

Frequently Asked: Is Living Donation Safe? Living kidney donation is a well-established and safe procedure. Donors undergo thorough medical and psychological evaluations to ensure they are healthy and fully informed. Most donors are able to return to their normal lives within weeks, and studies show that living with one kidney does not decrease life expectancy or quality of life. All medical costs related to the donation are typically covered by the recipient’s insurance, and support is available throughout the entire process. If you're curious or even just considering the possibility, exploring the information is the first step — no obligation, just understanding.
